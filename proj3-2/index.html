Part 1

Implementation

To implement this part, we followed the spec strictly. For the reflect function, we assigned wi to be the normal of wi. For mirror sample_f, we simply can reflect and turn reflectance / abs_cos_theta(*wi) because we need to cancel out the cosine that the at_least_one_bounce_radiance function will multiply by.

For refraction, we first find the proper eta based on whether the ray is entering or exiting the substance. Then we followed Snell’s law to determine whether we have total internal reflection--in this case, we returned false and the wi field is unused. For the other case, we assign wi to be the specified value. For sample_f, when refraction happens, we return transmittance / abs_cos_theta(*wi) / eta^2 where eta is determined in the same way as before.

For glass material, we determined the value of eta, calculated Schlick's approximation, and differentiated between the case of total internal reflection or refraction. For reflection or refraction, we probabilistically do the calculations, set the pdf, and return the corresponding values as before for the case of refraction/reflection.

Image Explanations

At ray depth = 0, because we do not have any bounces, we only see the direct light source, which is the light on the top and everything else is completely dark.

At ray depth = 1, we can now see the lighting applied to the walls and the reflection of the light on the balls. Because we only have this one bounce, areas not captured by direct lighting would be completely dark. For example, the shadows are black, and the balls do not reflect the walls yet.

At ray depth = 2, in addition to the previous lighting effects, the shadows are lit up and we can also see the reflections from the spheres. This makes sense considering we are now working with two bounces as those effects take exactly two bounces to accomplish. The effects that require more than two bounces, therefore, are not present. These include refractions and lit-up ceilings in the reflected images.

At ray depth = 3, because refractions and lit-up ceilings in the reflected images take exactly 3 bounces, they are the new effects that we get. Other effects that we had from depth = 2 are also there. The only part completely missing is the front sphere’s refraction effects reflected in the back sphere. This takes 4 bounces as we need one more step to reflect the refractions that take 3 steps.

At ray depth = 4, we now have the front sphere’s refraction effects reflected in the back sphere. As we discussed, this takes 4 bounces and is now achieved.

At ray depth = 5, the effects stay similar but we get a brighter image because essentially all the lights reflect into each other in a diminishing way, and thus the whole picture gets more vivid.

At ray depth = 100, the effects are still generally the same, but the image becomes even more bright and vivid since we have more reflecting bounces.

Part 4

Implementation

We cast a ray from the current pixel through the center of the lens and find the ray's intersection point with the image plane, pFocus. Then we sample a point on the lens, pLens. Finally, we create a ray that starts at pLens and goes in the direction of pFocus.

Differences between the pinhole and thin-lens camera models

A pinhole lens only allows light to pass through one point. For any given point on an object, there's only one way for that point to reach the sensor, so everything will be in focus. In contrast, in a thin-lens model, there are multiple ways for that same point to reach the sensor, and a point on the sensor can receive light from multiple places on the object. Therefore, not everything will be in focus with a thin-lens model. However, pinhole cameras don't exist in the real world and the depth of field effects created by thin-lens cameras can allow us to achieve unique effects by placing emphasis on subjects of interest.
